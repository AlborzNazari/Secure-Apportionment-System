THEORETICAL FOUNDATION

The Science Behind the Allocation

PRIORITY FORMULA
P(n) = V / √(n · (n+1))
V = total votes cast for party
n = seats currently held
P = priority score (max-heap key)
Each allocation round awards the next seat to the party with the highest P. The geometric mean √(n·(n+1)) minimises relative inequality between any two parties — the core guarantee.
MATHEMATICAL GUARANTEES
✓
House Monotonicity
Increasing total seats never causes a party to lose a seat. Hamilton's method violates this — the Alabama Paradox.
✓
Population Monotonicity
If party A grows faster than party B between elections, A never loses a seat to B.
✓
No Alabama Paradox
Proven by Balinski & Young (1982). Huntington-Hill is the unique method satisfying both monotonicity conditions.
⚠
Quota Violation Possible
May allocate seats outside the natural quota range in extreme cases. Unavoidable — Balinski-Young impossibility theorem.

ARROW'S IMPOSSIBILITY
No voting system with 3+ candidates can simultaneously satisfy all four fairness criteria.
Unanimity
If all voters prefer A over B, A must rank above B
IIA
Relative ranking of A vs B unaffected by C's presence
Non-dictatorship
No single voter determines the collective outcome
Transitivity
If A > B and B > C, then A > C must hold
